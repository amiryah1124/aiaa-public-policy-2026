## From a New Role to a Public Policy Platform

In June 2025, I began serving as the **Public Policy Officer** for the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section. This was the first time the Cape Canaveral Section had a dedicated officer for public policy, and because of that, the role had to be built with intention, patience, and a clear understanding of what public policy could mean for our local aerospace community.

At the national level, AIAA identifies several major advocacy and policy priorities that are directly connected to the future of aerospace. These include areas such as aviation emissions and sustainability, space traffic management and space situational awareness, STEM education, and workforce sustainment. These topics are not only policy issues; they are deeply connected to engineering, science, education, workforce development, and the future of the aerospace ecosystem.

From the beginning, the mission of the Public Policy role was to raise awareness and build connections. We wanted to help students, faculty, professionals, government representatives, industry leaders, and community members better understand how aerospace policy affects technical work, education, workforce needs, and public engagement.

However, it became clear very early that this mission could not be fulfilled by one person alone. Public policy is not a narrow topic, and aerospace public policy requires connections across many different communities. With the support and guidance of **Kevin Johnson**, Chair of the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section and an experienced aerospace leader in both industry and academia, we expanded the role from a single officer position into a broader **Public Policy Committee**.

To support this effort, **Joshua Robinett** joined as Secretary and **Alexander Killian** joined as Student Coordinator. Together, we began building a more structured approach to public policy outreach for the section. This committee became the foundation for a year-long effort focused on listening, learning, connecting, and ultimately creating a platform where different communities could meet and share their perspectives.

---

## Understanding the Gap Between Technical Work and Public Policy Awareness

The first stage of our work was not to organize an event immediately. Instead, we wanted to understand the landscape around us.

Beginning in summer 2025, we reached out to students and faculty in the Aerospace Engineering and Mechanical Engineering communities at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University. We wanted to understand how familiar they were with public policy in aerospace, whether they had done research connected to aerospace policy, and whether they were interested in exploring the connection between technical work and policy.

The responses were mixed, but they were very informative. Some students and faculty were interested but had not yet pursued research directly connected to public policy. Some were not particularly interested in policy. But one of the most important observations was that several people were already working on technical problems that had clear public policy relevance, even if they did not necessarily describe their work in those terms.

This was an important realization. It showed us that the gap was not always a lack of technical contribution. In many cases, the gap was a lack of awareness that technical work could have a policy dimension.

For example, research connected to aerospace sustainability, space operations, national security, aviation systems, workforce development, manufacturing, and emerging technologies can all intersect with public policy. Yet students and researchers may not always recognize that their work contributes to broader policy conversations.

This became one of the key motivations behind our initiative: to help technical communities see the broader impact of their work and to help policy-focused communities better understand the technical realities behind aerospace challenges.

---

## Building Bridges Across Campus

After these early conversations, we realized that part of our mission had to be connecting different communities within the university itself.

Engineering students and faculty often work on technical solutions, while other departments may focus more directly on policy, security, governance, regulation, and international affairs. These groups may exist on the same campus, but they do not always have structured opportunities to interact.

To better understand the policy-related expertise already present at Embry-Riddle, we began learning more about faculty and programs connected to **Security Studies and International Affairs**, **Astrophysics**, and **Homeland Security**. Through this process, we became more aware of the work and interests of professors such as **Dr. Elisabeth (Hope) Murray**, **Dr. Alice Dell’Era**, and **Dr. Trevor Simoneau**, whose areas of interest include environmental security, national and international security, space policy, and aviation legislation and regulation.

These connections helped us recognize that public policy in aerospace is inherently interdisciplinary. It is not only about government decisions, and it is not only about technical innovation. It exists at the intersection of engineering, governance, security, education, industry, and public service.

This insight shaped the structure of everything that followed.

## Expanding Beyond Campus: Community, Industry, and Government

As our work continued, we realized that public policy outreach could not remain only on campus. If our goal was to raise awareness and build meaningful connections, we needed to engage with the broader community as well.

Joshua Robinett and I began reaching out to different gatherings, conferences, and community groups to better understand who might be interested in aerospace and aviation policy outside the university environment.

One important moment came when we attended the **SmallSat Education Conference at Kennedy Space Center** in October 2025. This gave us the opportunity to meet educators, professionals, and leaders who care deeply about aerospace education and public engagement. During that conference, we connected with **Kevin Simmons**, the director of the conference.

Kevin Simmons became an important source of insight and encouragement. Through his years of experience, his connections, and his dedication to aerospace education, he helped us better understand public interest, industry needs, and the importance of connecting students with real aerospace opportunities. His suggestions and support helped us expand our outreach beyond campus and better understand where gaps existed between students, educators, industry, and the public.

We also benefited from conversations with **One Church Port Orange**, where **Pastor Nick Griffin** and **Pastor Jason Burnside** actively support students and help create community. We shared our public policy vision with them in both technical and non-technical terms so that the idea could reach students and community members we might not otherwise be able to reach directly.

Since our mission involved government and governance in space policy, this connection was very important. Having Representative Sirois involved gave us the opportunity to bring direct legislative and governmental insight into the conversation.

By the end of 2025, we had gathered meaningful input from four major communities:

* On-campus academic communities
* Off-campus community partners
* Industry and education leaders
* Government representatives

What we needed next was a way to bring these groups together.

## From Outreach to Action: The Idea for the Event

By December 2025, our data-gathering and outreach phase had reached a point where we could clearly see the need for a shared platform.

We had spoken with students, faculty, community leaders, industry-connected educators, and government representatives. Each group had something valuable to contribute, but there was no dedicated space where they could meet, exchange ideas, and understand one another's perspectives.

As a new committee, we also did not yet have a newsletter, dedicated communication channel, or established public policy platform within the section. We needed a way to move from private conversations and individual outreach into a shared community experience.

That is when the idea of the **AIAA Public Policy Outreach Event** emerged.

The goal was not simply to host a one-day event. The goal was to create a platform where the communities we had been reaching out to could finally meet, greet, connect, and share.

We used what we had learned from our interactions to decide where the event should be held, who should be invited to speak, what themes should be addressed, and how the day should be structured.

Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University was chosen as the host location because of its accessibility, its aerospace identity, and the strong interest among students and professionals in the region to connect through the university environment.

---

## Designing the Event: A Multi-Community Approach

The event was intentionally designed around the four communities we had identified during our outreach process: academia, industry and education, government, and the broader community.

![Official event poster](images/event_poster.jpg)

*Figure 2: Official event poster outlining the structure, speakers, and thematic focus of the AIAA Public Policy Outreach Event 2026.*

Our speakers were selected because each represented an important part of the public policy ecosystem we wanted to connect.

**Representative Tyler Sirois** represented the government and legislative perspective. His role allowed attendees to hear directly about current space policy priorities, legislative direction, and the role of government in advancing the space sector.

**Dr. Elisabeth (Hope) Murray** represented the academic and policy perspective. Her work connected directly to space policy, governance, environmental security, and strategic decision-making in the evolving space ecosystem.

**Kevin Simmons** represented the connection between industry, education, and workforce development. His experience helped frame the relationship between educational institutions, industry needs, workforce preparation, and student opportunity.

**Kevin Johnson** represented AIAA leadership, professional engagement, and community impact. As Chair of the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section, his perspective connected the mission of AIAA with public outreach, professional development, and support for the next generation of aerospace professionals.

The talks were not selected randomly. They were chosen to build upon one another, almost like chapters in a coherent book. The day was structured to begin with government, policy, and governance; then move toward industry, education, and workforce development; then conclude with professional engagement, community impact, and student research.

This structure helped connect public policy to technical work, education, professional societies, and community engagement.

---

## Why the Event Was Centered Around Students

Although the event was designed to benefit everyone involved, the central focus of the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section has always been students.

As we developed the event, we kept returning to one question: **Who are we doing this for?**

The answer was clear. We wanted students to benefit directly. We wanted them to be seen, heard, recognized, and connected. We did not want students to simply sit in the audience and listen. We wanted them to be part of the event.

This led to the decision to include a dedicated **Research & Innovation Poster Symposium** as a major component of the day.

To make this possible, we reached out to the **Office of Undergraduate Research at ERAU** and connected with **Ryan Reynolds**. With his support, we added a student poster presentation section where students could share their latest research, discuss their work with professionals, and build connections with people from academia, industry, government, and the community.

The response was very encouraging. We received approximately **40 student posters**, demonstrating the strength and diversity of student research at Embry-Riddle.

The poster symposium also served a deeper purpose. It helped students recognize that their technical research may have broader relevance to aerospace policy, workforce needs, sustainability, security, governance, and community impact. It created a space where students could begin to see their work not only as academic projects, but as contributions to the future of aerospace.

In addition to the poster symposium, we invited **Margaret O'Brien**, whose research was especially connected to space policy, to serve as a student speaker. Her presentation reinforced one of the central messages of the event: students are not only future contributors to aerospace policy and innovation; they are already part of the conversation.

## Event Program and Agenda

Given the scope of the mission, the event became a full-day program held from **9:00 AM to 4:00 PM** on **May 1, 2026**, National Space Day.

The event agenda was designed to support both formal learning and informal connection. The day included networking breakfast, opening remarks, speaker sessions, student research presentation, lunch networking, a closing keynote, and the research poster symposium.

[View Event Agenda](materials/agenda.pdf)

The formal agenda included three major sessions:

1. **Government, Policy & Governance in Space**
2. **Industry, Education & Workforce Development**
3. **Professional Engagement & Community Impact**

This structure reflected the outreach process that shaped the event. We wanted the audience to move from policy and governance, to workforce and education, to professional engagement and student-centered impact.

## Event Day: Bringing the Vision to Life

The event began at 9:00 AM with a networking breakfast. This was not just a logistical opening; it was an important part of the event design.

Before the formal talks began, attendees had the opportunity to meet one another, introduce themselves, and begin conversations across different backgrounds. Students, faculty, professionals, community members, and invited speakers were able to connect informally before the program moved into its structured sessions.

This early networking helped set the tone for the day. The atmosphere was open, welcoming, and collaborative. It reflected exactly what the committee had hoped to create: a space where people from different communities could begin speaking with one another.

The event officially opened with welcoming remarks and an introduction to AIAA, the objectives of the event, and the structure of the day. The opening emphasized the importance of connecting public policy awareness with aerospace education, research, professional development, and community engagement.

---

## Session 1: Government, Policy, and Governance in Space

The first session focused on government, policy, and governance in space.

Representative Tyler Sirois opened the main speaker program by providing a government perspective. His talk addressed current space policy priorities, legislative direction, and the role of government in advancing the space sector. This session helped attendees understand that aerospace innovation does not happen in isolation. It is shaped by policy decisions, public investment, regulation, infrastructure, and long-term strategic planning.

Following this, Dr. Elisabeth (Hope) Murray discussed space policy and governance from an academic and strategic perspective. Her talk focused on how policy frameworks, global governance, and strategic decision-making shape the evolving space ecosystem. This expanded the conversation from local and legislative priorities to broader questions of governance, security, international cooperation, and long-term responsibility in space.

Together, these two talks established the foundation for the day. They showed that aerospace policy is not a separate topic from aerospace engineering. It is one of the environments in which aerospace engineering operates.

---

## Session 2: Industry, Education, and Workforce Development

The second session shifted the focus toward industry, education, and workforce development.

Kevin Simmons discussed industry and education initiatives, emphasizing workforce development, industry needs, and collaboration between private sector organizations and educational institutions. His perspective helped connect policy and governance to the practical question of how we prepare students for the future aerospace workforce.

This session was especially important because workforce development is one of the strongest bridges between students, universities, industry, and public policy. Aerospace policy is not only about regulation or legislation; it is also about whether the next generation is prepared to meet future technical, operational, and societal challenges.

The student research presentation followed this session and helped bring the conversation back to students as active contributors. This reinforced the idea that student research, when properly recognized and connected, can be part of the larger aerospace policy and workforce conversation.

---

## Lunch and Networking

After the morning sessions, attendees gathered for lunch and networking.

This part of the day was intentionally included to give participants time to speak more directly with speakers, sponsors, students, and peers. Many of the most valuable outcomes of an outreach event happen outside formal presentations, during conversations where people exchange ideas, ask questions, and discover shared interests.

For students, this was an opportunity to engage with professionals and leaders in a more personal setting. For speakers and community members, it was an opportunity to learn more about student interests, university research, and local aerospace engagement.

---

## Session 3: Professional Engagement and Community Impact

The third session focused on professional engagement and community impact.

Kevin Johnson spoke about AIAA and community impact, emphasizing the role of professional organizations in policy engagement, public outreach, and supporting the next generation of aerospace professionals. His talk connected the mission of the event to the broader mission of AIAA as a professional society.

This session was important because it showed that public policy engagement does not only happen through government offices or formal institutions. It also happens through professional communities, volunteer leadership, mentorship, outreach, and sustained service.

The event then moved into a closing keynote delivered by **Joshua Robinett**. His closing remarks brought together the major themes of the day: policy, innovation, collaboration, education, workforce development, and student engagement. Joshua provided a forward-looking perspective on how these elements can work together to support the future of aerospace.

His keynote served as a meaningful conclusion to the formal speaker program. It helped summarize the day not as separate talks, but as a connected story about how aerospace communities can work together more intentionally.

---

## Research and Innovation Poster Symposium

After the formal sessions and a networking break, the event transitioned into the **Research & Innovation Poster Symposium**.

This was one of the most important parts of the event because it placed students at the center of the day.

Students and researchers presented their work across aerospace engineering, policy, and related fields. Attendees had the opportunity to move through the poster area, ask questions, provide feedback, and learn about current student research.

The symposium created visibility for students and gave them a platform to present their work in front of an audience that included faculty, professionals, community members, and policy-minded leaders. It also allowed attendees to see the depth and range of student work taking place at Embry-Riddle.

Most importantly, the symposium helped connect student research to the larger themes of the event. Many research projects may begin as technical studies, but they can also have relevance to sustainability, safety, workforce development, national security, aviation systems, space operations, and public policy.

## Attendance and Community Response

The event brought together approximately **80 attendees** from academia, industry, government, student organizations, and the broader community.

For a new committee and a first-time public policy initiative within the section, this level of engagement was meaningful. It showed that there is real interest in connecting aerospace technical work with public policy awareness, especially when the topic is presented in an accessible and community-oriented way.

The attendance also reflected the value of the outreach work that took place throughout the previous year. The event was not built only through announcements or invitations. It was built through conversations, relationships, and the gradual development of trust across different communities.

---

## Impact and Outcomes

The AIAA Public Policy Outreach Event 2026 achieved several important outcomes.

It created a platform where students, faculty, government representatives, industry-connected educators, professional society leaders, and community members could meet in one place.

It helped raise awareness of aerospace public policy and showed how policy connects to engineering, education, workforce development, sustainability, space governance, and community engagement.

It gave students a direct role in the event through the student speaker opportunity and the Research & Innovation Poster Symposium.

It strengthened the role of the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section as a bridge between technical professionals, students, policy conversations, and the broader aerospace community.

It also demonstrated that public policy outreach can be practical, inclusive, and student-centered. Public policy does not have to remain abstract or distant. It can be brought into direct conversation with research, education, professional development, and local community engagement.

It began with a new role and a simple mission: to raise awareness of public policy in aerospace and connect communities that do not always have the opportunity to speak with one another.

Over time, that mission became a committee, then a network of relationships, then a full-day public event centered on students, policy, education, industry, government, and community engagement.

This initiative represents a starting point. It shows that there is strong potential for continued public policy engagement within the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section and the broader aerospace community.

Moving forward, the goal is to continue building platforms that help students recognize the broader impact of their work, help professionals engage with policy conversations, and help communities understand how aerospace innovation connects to public service and the future of society.
