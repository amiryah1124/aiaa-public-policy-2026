## From a New Role to a Public Policy Platform

In June 2025, I began serving as the **Public Policy Officer** for the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section. This was the first time the section had a dedicated public policy role, which made the first year an opportunity to define the mission, build awareness, and create a foundation for long-term engagement.

AIAA’s national advocacy priorities include issues such as aviation emissions and sustainability, space traffic management and space situational awareness, STEM education, and workforce sustainment. These topics are not separate from aerospace research and engineering; they are directly connected to the future of the aerospace ecosystem.

> **Mission:** Raise awareness of aerospace public policy and connect students, faculty, professionals, industry leaders, government representatives, and the broader community.

With the support and guidance of **Kevin Johnson**, Chair of the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section, the role expanded from a single officer position into a **Public Policy Committee**. The committee included:

* **Amir Yahyaeian** — Public Policy Chair
* **Joshua Robinett** — Secretary
* **Alexander Killian** — Student Coordinator

Together, we began a year-long effort focused on outreach, listening, and community building.

---

## Understanding the Gap

The first stage of the initiative was not to organize an event immediately. It was to understand the landscape around us.

Beginning in summer 2025, we reached out to students and faculty at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University, especially within the Aerospace Engineering and Mechanical Engineering communities, to better understand how public policy was perceived in aerospace research and education.

A key insight emerged:

> Many students and faculty were already working on technical problems with strong public policy relevance, but they did not always recognize their work from that perspective.

This gap was not always a lack of technical contribution. In many cases, it was a lack of connection between technical research and broader policy awareness.

Examples of technical areas with public policy relevance include:

* Aerospace sustainability
* Space operations and governance
* National and international security
* Aviation systems and regulation
* Workforce development
* Advanced manufacturing and emerging technologies

This became one of the main motivations behind the initiative: to help technical communities see the broader impact of their work and to help policy-focused communities better understand the technical realities behind aerospace challenges.

---

## Building Bridges Across Campus and Beyond

As our outreach continued, we realized that the public policy mission required connecting multiple communities.

On campus, this meant building bridges between engineering groups and departments connected to policy, security, governance, regulation, and international affairs. Through this process, we became more familiar with work connected to:

* Security Studies and International Affairs
* Astrophysics
* Homeland Security

We also learned more about the work and interests of faculty such as **Dr. Elisabeth (Hope) Murray**, **Dr. Alice Dell’Era**, and **Dr. Trevor Simoneau**, whose areas include environmental security, national and international security, space policy, and aviation legislation and regulation.

Beyond campus, Joshua Robinett and I attended the **SmallSat Education Conference at Kennedy Space Center** in October 2025. This became an important step in understanding broader public interest, industry needs, and student opportunity in aerospace education.

![Amir Yah and Joshua Robinett at Kennedy Space Center](images/smallsat_ksc.jpg)

*Figure 1: Amir Yah (left) and Joshua Robinett (right) at Kennedy Space Center during the SmallSat Education Conference, October 2025.*

At the conference, we connected with **Kevin Simmons**, whose leadership, experience, and network helped us better understand the connection between aerospace education, workforce development, and public engagement.

We also benefited from conversations with **One Church Port Orange**, where **Pastor Nick Griffin** and **Pastor Jason Burnside** actively support students and community-building efforts. Sharing our public policy vision with them helped extend the conversation beyond the university environment.

At the government level, Kevin Johnson encouraged us to connect with **Representative Tyler Sirois** from the Florida House of Representatives. Since our mission involved government and governance in space policy, this connection brought an important legislative perspective into the initiative.

By the end of 2025, our outreach had connected four major communities:

* On-campus academic groups
* Off-campus community partners
* Industry and education leaders
* Government representatives

The next step was to bring these communities together.

## From Outreach to Action

By December 2025, it became clear that there was no unified platform where these communities could meet, engage, and understand one another’s perspectives.

> **The need:** Create a shared space where aerospace, policy, education, workforce development, and community engagement could come together.

This led to the idea for the **AIAA Public Policy Outreach Event 2026**.

The goal was not simply to host a one-day program. The goal was to create a platform shaped by nearly a year of outreach and relationship-building.

Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University was selected as the host location because of its accessibility, aerospace identity, and strong student-centered environment.

---

## Designing the Event: A Multi-Community Approach

The event was intentionally designed around the four communities identified through our outreach: academia, industry and education, government, and the broader community.

![Official event poster](images/event_poster.jpg)

*Figure 2: Official event poster outlining the structure, speakers, and thematic focus of the AIAA Public Policy Outreach Event 2026.*

The speakers were selected to represent different parts of the aerospace public policy ecosystem:

* **Representative Tyler Sirois** — Government and legislative perspective
* **Dr. Elisabeth (Hope) Murray** — Space policy, governance, and strategic decision-making
* **Kevin Simmons** — Industry, education, and workforce development
* **Kevin Johnson** — AIAA leadership, professional engagement, and community impact

The program was structured so that each session built on the previous one:

1. **Government, Policy & Governance in Space**
2. **Industry, Education & Workforce Development**
3. **Professional Engagement & Community Impact**
4. **Research & Innovation Poster Symposium**

The intention was for the event to feel less like a set of separate talks and more like a connected story about aerospace, policy, education, and people.

---

## Why Students Were Central

Although the event was designed to benefit everyone involved, the central focus of the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section has always been students.

As the event developed, one question guided the planning:

> **Who are we doing this for?**

The answer was clear: students needed to be more than attendees. They needed to be seen, heard, recognized, and connected.

This led to the creation of a dedicated **Research & Innovation Poster Symposium**. With support from **Ryan Reynolds** and the **Office of Undergraduate Research at ERAU**, students were invited to present their latest research and engage directly with attendees from academia, industry, government, and the community.

The event included approximately **40 student posters**, demonstrating the depth and diversity of student research at Embry-Riddle.

The symposium served two important purposes:

* It gave students visibility and professional engagement.
* It helped students recognize how technical research can connect to broader issues such as policy, sustainability, security, governance, workforce development, and community impact.

In addition, **Margaret O’Brien**, whose research was closely connected to space policy, was invited to serve as the student speaker. Her presentation reinforced one of the central messages of the event:

> Students are not only future contributors to aerospace policy and innovation; they are already part of the conversation.

## Event Day: Program and Discussion

The event was held from **9:00 AM to 4:00 PM** on **May 1, 2026**, National Space Day.

The day began with a **networking breakfast**, giving students, faculty, speakers, professionals, and community members an opportunity to connect before the formal sessions. This early networking helped set an open and collaborative tone for the day.

The program officially opened with welcoming remarks, an introduction to AIAA, the objectives of the event, and an overview of the day.

### Session 1: Government, Policy, and Governance in Space

The first session focused on how government, policy, and governance shape the aerospace sector.

* **Representative Tyler Sirois** provided a government perspective on current space policy priorities, legislative direction, and the role of government in advancing the space sector.
* **Dr. Elisabeth (Hope) Murray** discussed policy frameworks, global governance, and strategic decision-making in the evolving space ecosystem.

Together, these talks established the foundation for the day and showed that aerospace policy is not separate from aerospace engineering; it is one of the environments in which aerospace engineering operates.

### Session 2: Industry, Education, and Workforce Development

The second session shifted the focus toward workforce development and the relationship between education and industry.

* **Kevin Simmons** discussed industry needs, workforce development, and collaboration between private sector organizations and educational institutions.
* A **student research presentation** brought the discussion back to students as active contributors to the aerospace ecosystem.

This session emphasized that aerospace policy is not only about regulation or legislation. It is also about preparing the next generation to meet future technical, operational, and societal challenges.

### Lunch and Networking

Lunch provided time for attendees to speak directly with speakers, sponsors, students, and peers.

Many of the most valuable outcomes of an outreach event happen outside formal presentations, through conversations where people exchange ideas, ask questions, and discover shared interests.

### Session 3: Professional Engagement and Community Impact

The third session focused on professional engagement and the role of AIAA in supporting the aerospace community.

* **Kevin Johnson** discussed AIAA and community impact, emphasizing the role of professional organizations in policy engagement, public outreach, and support for the next generation of aerospace professionals.
* **Joshua Robinett** delivered the closing keynote, bringing together the major themes of the day: policy, innovation, collaboration, education, workforce development, and student engagement.

Joshua’s closing remarks helped frame the day not as separate talks, but as a connected story about how aerospace communities can work together more intentionally.

---

## Research and Innovation Poster Symposium

After the formal sessions and a networking break, the event transitioned into the **Research & Innovation Poster Symposium**.

This was one of the most important parts of the event because it placed students at the center of the day.

Students and researchers presented their work across aerospace engineering, policy, and related fields. Attendees moved through the poster area, asked questions, provided feedback, and learned about current student research.

The symposium helped connect student research to the larger themes of the event. Many research projects begin as technical studies, but they may also have relevance to sustainability, safety, workforce development, national security, aviation systems, space operations, and public policy.

## Impact and Outcomes

The AIAA Public Policy Outreach Event 2026 achieved several important outcomes:

* Brought together approximately **80 attendees** from academia, industry, government, student organizations, and the broader community
* Featured approximately **40 student posters** through the Research & Innovation Poster Symposium
* Created a platform for dialogue between technical, policy, education, industry, government, and community perspectives
* Increased awareness of aerospace public policy within the student and academic community
* Highlighted the policy relevance of technical research
* Strengthened the role of the AIAA Cape Canaveral Section as a bridge between students, professionals, policy conversations, and the broader aerospace community
* Demonstrated that public policy outreach can be practical, inclusive, and student-centered

> Public policy does not have to remain abstract or distant. It can be brought into direct conversation with research, education, professional development, and local community engagement.

## Looking Forward

The AIAA Public Policy Outreach Event 2026 was more than a one-day program. It was the result of nearly a year of outreach, learning, relationship-building, and teamwork.

It began with a new role and a simple mission: to raise awareness of public policy in aerospace and connect communities that do not always have the opportunity to speak with one another.

Over time, that mission became a committee, then a network of relationships, then a full-day public event centered on students, policy, education, industry, government, and community engagement.

Moving forward, the goal is to continue building platforms that help students recognize the broader impact of their work, help professionals engage with policy conversations, and help communities understand how aerospace innovation connects to public service and the future of society.
